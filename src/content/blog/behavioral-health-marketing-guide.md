Marketing a behavioral health practice is not like marketing a restaurant. You are reaching people in a vulnerable moment, you are bound by privacy and advertising rules, and the stakes of getting it wrong are real. After six years marketing for behavioral health, here is the practical playbook we trust.

## Start with intake, not impressions

In behavioral health, the metric that matters is qualified intake: the right clients, at the right level of care, who actually start treatment. Vanity metrics like reach or follower counts are a distraction. Build your tracking around inquiries, scheduled assessments, and admits, and judge every channel against those.

## Respect the rules from day one

Behavioral health advertising carries real compliance requirements, from HIPAA-conscious tracking to platform policies for sensitive categories. A few non-negotiables:

- Avoid passing protected health information into ad or analytics tools.
- Use compliant, privacy-aware conversion tracking.
- Keep claims honest and outcomes realistic. Trust is your whole business.

## Lead with trust, not pressure

People choosing care are evaluating safety and credibility before price. The content that converts is the content that reassures: clear descriptions of your approach, your team's credentials, what the first call is like, and answers to the fears people actually have. Plain, human language beats clinical jargon every time.

## The channels that tend to work

- **Local SEO and Google Business Profile** for families searching "[service] near me."
- **Search ads** on high-intent terms, paired with a calm, specific landing page.
- **Content** that answers real questions, which also builds the organic authority that compounds over time.

## Measure what matters, honestly

Tie spend to inquiries and admits, review it weekly, and shift budget toward what is genuinely working. The goal is sustainable, ethical growth, not a spike that burns trust.
